Starlit Garage Band

Mechanics by day, rooftop band by night. They tune engines and heartstrings before their first tour bus ever leaves the alley.

Found family Late-night jams Soft grunge

Story

The garage smelled like oil, citrus cleaner, and possibility. June wiped grease from her cheek with the back of her hand and strummed a chord to see if the amp still buzzed. It did.

“We fix engines, we fix sound,” Malik said, tightening a bolt on the drum kit made from repurposed hubcaps. Their laughter bounced off concrete walls, too big for the room.

After midnight, they climbed to the roof. String lights tangled around old antennae, city lights blinking like they were keeping tempo. Theo unpacked a notebook. “I wrote something on the bus ride,” he offered, shy as a first chord.

They played until neighbors opened windows instead of filing complaints. The song wasn’t polished, but it was honest: a chorus about engines that don’t quit and people who choose each other when the world doesn’t. June’s voice cracked on the last line; no one minded.

When the music faded, they sat in the warm hush that follows a good set. “Tour someday?” Malik asked.

“We’re already moving,” Theo said, nodding at the stars. The night smelled like rain and new starts, and for the first time, the alley felt wide enough for their dreams.
